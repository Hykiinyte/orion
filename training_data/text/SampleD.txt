Mathematics is a core principle when it comes to understanding the entire universe. At its base, mathematics comes from numbers. Numbers include 0 or zero, 1 or one, 2 or two, 3 or three, 4 or four, 5 or five, six or 6, 7 or seven, 8 or eight, and 9 or nine. These numbers can then combine to represent numbers greater than the zero to nine range such as 10 or ten, which denotes 1 greater than 9. With numbers, you can now assign values to anything such as having 2 pineapples, 70 dollars, or a single sheet of paper. Once you have numbers, the next step is to create operations, which allow you to change and manipulate numbers using each other. Addition and subtraction are the first mathematical operations many will learn in school. Addition indicates the increase of a value by an amount. For example, 3 added to 2, or simply three plus two, or "3+2" indicates the combined value of 3 and 2, which is 5. Therefore three plus two equals five, or in mathematical terms, "3+2=5". Subtraction is the inverse function or opposite of addition. Subtraction denotes a decrease of a value by an amount. Three less than five equals two, or in mathematical terms it can be written as "5-3=2". These two functions are the most fundamental operations of all mathematics which nearly everything relies upon. The next operations are multiplication and division. Multiplication denotes a count of groups of items. For example, seven groups of three, or "7*3", which equals twenty-one or 21. It can also be written as "3+3+3+3+3+3+3=21". Multiplication allows repeated addition to be simplified into a simpler equation. The inverse to the multiplication function is division, which separates a value into groups of equal items. So if you take your previous example of "7*3=21", division can rewrite it as twenty-one separates into seven groups of three, or "21/7=3". As a repeated subtraction function, it will be "21-3-3-3-3-3-3-3=7". Using the same idea of repeating functions, we get exponents and radicals which are products of repeated multiplication and division respectively. Four with a exponent of four or 4^4 would equal two-hundred-fifty-six or 256. "4^4" is equal to "4*4*4*4" or four raised to the fourth power. Radicals represent repeated division, and are the inverse function of exponents. These are the fundamentals of mathematics, and these operations allow for mathematical logic and numerical values to be measured and manipulated. However, things get dicey when we add organization. For example, 2+2*4 can equal either 10 if you first do 2*4=8 then 2+8=10, or alternatively 16 if you do 2+2=4 then 4*4=16. This is where the order of operations comes in, when you do PEMDAS. PEMDAS is short of Parenthesis, Exponents, Multiplication, Division, Addition, Subtraction. Using PEMDAS, the equation 2+2*4 equals 16 since multiplication comes first, so we do 2*4=8, then 2+8=10. Alternatively, if we add parenthesis as (2+2)*4, we do 2+2 first which equals 4, then 4*4=16, therefore the answer with order of operations is 16. There is an alternative to PEMDAS written as BODMAS or Brackets, Orders, Division, Multiplication, Addition, Subtraction, which is fundamentally the same thing except it is referred to as such in different regions. The order of operations respects ordering and organization first, then multiplication and division, then lastly addition and subtraction.
